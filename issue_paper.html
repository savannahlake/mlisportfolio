Issue Paper

As digital collections and digitization projects grow, so does the use of optical character recognition (OCR) technologies to enable keyword searching of scanned newspapers, manuscripts, and other text-based materials. However, OCR capabilities can be extremely limited depending on the source material it is reading, with accuracy ratings being as low as 10-40% depending on the clarity of the image, the size of the text, if the image is in a different language, and if the text is handwritten. Sometimes an entire digital library is OCR’d (at different accuracy rates by collection) and sometimes only certain collections are OCR’d—which is all to say that there are great inconsistencies in the technology itself as well as its application across a digital library.

Currently, there is not a clear understanding from the user’s perspective of what OCR technology is, how inconsistently it is applied across collections, and how that could affect their search results. This issue is exacerbated by the prominence of keyword search in digital collections. To a user, the search bar in a digital collection recalls a Google interface. But keyword searching in a digital collection is a far cry from a Google search—Google itself has not relied on keyword searches alone to retrieve results for years. And digital collections especially are unable to facilitate meaningful or consistent keyword searches given the limitations of OCR technologies. The false association between a search bar and Google leads users to be overly confident the capacity of a keyword search to retrieve complete and accurate results within a digital collection.

This paper will explore how the prominence of keyword search bars within digital collections combined with the limited capabilities of OCR technologies have perpetuated an environment of information illiteracy within digital collections. This paper will include a survey of the current OCR landscape, including its capabilities and limitations. It will also identify issues that should be directly communicated to users in order to increase user information literacy. And finally, this paper will explore more generative alternatives to keyword searching in digital collections, with the ultimate goal of making users more effective in navigating and understanding digital collections.
